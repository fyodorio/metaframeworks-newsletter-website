## The Good

The best thing I have found to share in the beginning of the year is the amount of new shiny stuff (_that is, releases_) among the metaframework creators. It is the time of exciting alpha and beta announcements, thus this usually secondary topic is the hero of today's "The Good" section.

Daishi Kato and the team had published [the alpha version of the first Waku's stable release](https://waku.gg/blog/waku-v1-alpha). If you wanted to give a try to this arguably the simplest way of playing with React Server Components, this is the sign!

> Our public API surface area is now stable as we shift focus towards bug fixes and compatibility.

RedwoodSDK gradually approaches its first stable release too with [lots of work being done](https://github.com/redwoodjs/sdk/releases/tag/v1.0.0-beta.48) almost on a daily basis.

Qwik team is closer and closer to [the long-awaited second version](https://qwik.dev/blog/qwik-2-coming-soon/) slowly but consistently pushing [new updates](https://github.com/QwikDev/qwik/releases/tag/%40qwik.dev%2Fcore%402.0.0-beta.17).

> The new Astro 6 development server refactor brings Astro‚Äôs development and production codepaths much closer together and increases stability for Astro on all runtimes.

## The Bad

And while someone is happy with the rise of the frameworks and tools, someone is always doubtful about this complexity wave.

In their [piece on anti-frameworkism](https://blog.logrocket.com/anti-frameworkism-native-web-apis), Anna Monus suggests an alternative view on starting new projects for the web today, which definitely makes sense and is supported by authoritative success stories.

> Anti-frameworkism isn‚Äôt about rejecting tools. It‚Äôs about starting from the problem instead of the trend, weighing real-world impact before convenience, and choosing the technology that best serves your users.

And it is not the one and only recent rant on chicken and egg problem in the web development of 2026 ‚Äî as [here in the post called "Avoid Mini-frameworks"](https://laike9m.com/blog/avoid-mini-frameworks,171/) the author also researches the idea of building blocks and resulting constructions.

Interestingly, Ryan Carniato [basically echoes this discourse by diving deeper](https://dev.to/this-is-learning/javascript-frameworks-heading-into-2026-2hel) in his already traditional and more specific and practical piece on JavaScript frameworks in the new year.

> I suppose after three years, we can consider my review of JavaScript Frameworks an annual event now.

To unofficially support that, Alicia Sykes [builds a bunch of apps in different frameworks](https://dev.to/lissy93/i-built-an-app-in-every-frontend-framework-4a9g) helping you to see for yourself.

Mark Techson from the Angular dev team also [gives some thoughtful predictions](https://podrocket.logrocket.com/angular-v21-signals-to-testing-with-mark-techson) on frameworks wars closer to the end of another recent PodRocket podcast episode dedicated to Angular (_but not only that_). Spoiler alert: the wars are over and the winner is... ü§´

And while everyone argues on what is the weapon of choice for the better, adversaries silently proceed conquering underlying `npm` ecosystem for worse. The amount of FUD brought by supply chain attacks in 2025 is overwhelmingly high. Only the last month yielded [Lotusbail vulnerability](https://www.securityweek.com/npm-package-with-56000-downloads-steals-whatsapp-credentials-data/) stealing WhatsApp credentials and data, [Evilginx phishing attack](https://thehackernews.com/2025/12/27-malicious-npm-packages-used-as.html?m=1) hunting for Microsoft login credentials, [jsPDF flaw](https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/critical-jspdf-flaw-lets-hackers-steal-secrets-via-generated-pdfs/) allowing hackers to snitch local secrets via PDF (_this one hurt me badly_), multiple React Router and Remix [vulnerability findings](https://bsky.app/profile/reactrouter.com/post/3mbwtrea3r224), and even [more foundational Node issues](https://nodejs.org/en/blog/vulnerability/january-2026-dos-mitigation-async-hooks) impacting several tools, frameworks, and metaframeworks.

The industry and web dev community are still looking for the best ways to mitigate that starting from [intermittently successful official measures](https://socket.dev/blog/npm-to-implement-staged-publishing), through [wise unofficial suggestions](https://humanwhocodes.com/blog/2026/01/how-github-could-secure-npm/), and to [pain-induced practical guerilla insights](https://nesbitt.io/2026/01/10/16-best-practices-for-reducing-dependabot-noise.html).

The only solid conclusion out of that is that the problems do not end when you did your research and chosen your tools and your building materials. Now the most interesting stage starts ‚Äî making sure they won't break by themselves or from external influence.
